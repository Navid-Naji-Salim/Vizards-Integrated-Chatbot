The Delal Bridge, also known as Pira Delal, is a historic stone arch bridge located in the city of Zakho in northern Iraq, spanning the Khabur River. It is believed to date back to the Roman period, though some historians argue it may have been constructed or rebuilt during the medieval era under local Kurdish rulers, which makes its exact origin somewhat debated. The bridge is made primarily of large limestone blocks and features a single large arch with smaller supporting arches, designed to withstand strong river currents and seasonal flooding. It has served for centuries as an important crossing point along trade and travel routes connecting Mesopotamia with Anatolia, making it strategically and economically significant in the past. Architecturally, the bridge is notable for its pointed arch and the precision of its stonework, which has allowed it to remain standing for hundreds, possibly over a thousand years. There are also local legends associated with it, including stories about its construction involving sacrifice or tragedy, which is common with many ancient bridges in the region. Today, Delal Bridge is one of the most recognizable landmarks in Zakho and the Kurdistan Region, attracting tourists and locals alike, especially because of its scenic setting over the river and surrounding greenery, and it continues to function as a pedestrian crossing while also holding cultural and historical importance for the area.
